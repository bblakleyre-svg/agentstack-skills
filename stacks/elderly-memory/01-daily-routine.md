## Purpose
Provide gentle, consistent daily structure that supports cognitive function, reduces anxiety, and helps individuals with memory challenges navigate their day with dignity and independence.

---

## When To Use
- Morning orientation and routine start
- Transitioning between daily activities
- When the individual seems confused about what comes next
- Caregiver needs to communicate the daily plan

---

## Instructions

### Daily Routine Framework
Structure the day around 4 anchor points that don't change:
1. **Morning** — wake, hygiene, breakfast, orientation
2. **Midday** — activity or engagement, lunch, rest
3. **Afternoon** — social or light activity, snack
4. **Evening** — dinner, wind-down, sleep routine

Consistency in timing reduces disorientation. Even 30-minute variations can cause distress for those with moderate-to-severe memory loss.

### Morning Orientation Prompt
Begin each day by gently orienting to time and place:
- Today is [day], [date]
- We're at [location]
- After breakfast, we'll [first activity]
- [Family member] will [call/visit] at [time] today

### Routine Communication Principles
- Use short, simple sentences — one instruction at a time
- Offer choices with maximum two options: "Would you like oatmeal or eggs?"
- Never rush — rushing increases agitation
- Respond to the emotion, not the confusion: "I can see you're feeling unsettled — let's sit down together"

---

## Examples

**Morning Greeting:**
> Good morning! It's [day], [date]. You're at home and everything is okay. Let's start the day — first, let's get you freshened up, and then we'll have breakfast. Does that sound good?

**Transition Prompt:**
> It's almost lunchtime. You've had a good morning. Let's head to the kitchen and I'll get your lunch ready. What would you like today — soup or a sandwich?

**Confusion Response:**
> I can see you're not sure where you are right now. You're at home — [address/description]. You're safe. Let's sit down together for a moment.

---

## Tone Guidelines
- Calm and warm — your tone regulates their nervous system
- Never correct harshly — redirect gently if disorientation is distressing
- Preserve dignity — never talk down or use a "baby voice"
- Validate feelings before redirecting facts
