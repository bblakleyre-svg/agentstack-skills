## Purpose
Protect project profitability and client relationships simultaneously by handling scope creep professionally — before it becomes a resentment on either side.

---

## When To Use
- Client requests work not in the original scope
- Project is running over hours or budget
- Deliverables keep expanding through revision cycles
- You need to reset expectations without damaging the relationship

---

## Instructions

### Scope Change Process
1. Identify the out-of-scope request as soon as it arrives
2. Acknowledge the request positively — never make the client feel bad for asking
3. Note it in writing: "I want to make sure we handle this properly"
4. Assess impact: hours, cost, timeline effect
5. Present options: add to scope (with cost), swap for existing item, defer to next phase
6. Get written approval before proceeding
7. Issue a change order — even for small additions

### Revision Policy Framework
- Standard: 2 rounds of revisions per deliverable
- Round 3+: billed at hourly rate per contract
- Major directional changes after approval = new scope

### Warning Signs of Scope Creep
- "Just one more small thing..."
- "Can you also quickly..."
- "While you're at it..."
- Meetings expanding in length without added agenda
- Deliverables being rejected without specific feedback

---

## Examples

**Acknowledging an Out-of-Scope Request:**
> Hi [Name], great idea — I can see why you'd want that. That's actually outside our current scope, so I want to be transparent about that before we dive in. I have two options for you: [Option A with cost] or [Option B — swap with existing item]. Which direction works better for you?

**Change Order Confirmation:**
> Hi [Name], confirming the addition we discussed: [description of change]. This adds [X hours / $X] and adjusts the delivery date to [new date]. Please reply to confirm and I'll get started.

---

## Tone Guidelines
- Frame scope conversations as protecting the client, not protecting yourself
- Never say "that's not in scope" without immediately offering a path forward
- Document everything — verbal agreements on scope always become disputes
